# UUA "Vision, Mission, and Covenant: Creating a Future Together" — Synthesis

Source: UUA, *Vision, Mission, and Covenant: Creating a Future Together* (New Congregation and Growth Resources, 2005). 98 pages. PDF in [`../_originals/UUA_Vision_Mission_Covenant_booklet.pdf`](../_originals/UUA_Vision_Mission_Covenant_booklet.pdf).

This is the canonical UUA reference for vision/mission/covenant work. The condensation below pulls forward the parts most relevant to **a vision-only project under tight meeting time** (CUUC's situation), and skips theological/historical material covered elsewhere in this folder.

## The booklet's process stages (page 19)

> *"Although we refer to vision, mission, and covenant together, it is possible for a congregation to choose to create only one or two of the statements."* — Tip box, p.19

We are doing vision only. CUUC already has the other four anchor documents. The full UUA stages are:

1. **Creating the team**
2. **Understanding the change process**
3. **Selling the vision/mission/covenant process** (to leadership and key informal influencers)
4. **Planning the process**
5. **Creating the vision** ← our main work
6. Creating the mission *(skip — already exists)*
7. Creating the covenant *(skip — already exists)*
8. **Implementation / following through**
9. **Evaluation**
10. **Revisiting**

> *"There is little positive to be gained by going through the development stage without the implementation, evaluation, and revisiting stages. Individuals who work hard only to see their results stored away on rolled-up newsprint in a closet will be hard-pressed to enter into other programs and processes of change."* — p.20

## Specific guidance worth keeping handy

### Team selection (p.20)
- Open to new ideas, work well with others, well-respected.
- Can work in consensus; capable of self-assertion *and* compromise.
- Balanced across areas of interest, longevity of membership, leadership experience.
- Professional leadership (minister, RE) *can* be on the team — but it is **often better not to have them chair**, "as the professional leadership often have enough on their plates."

### Change process (pp.22–25)
- Vision work is **adaptive change**, not technical. *"It may be tempting to simplify these kinds of issues and identify them as needing a technical solution. Sentences about these matters can begin with 'It's just a matter of …' or 'If we only do …' The risk is that it may gloss over what people hold most dear."* (p.25)
- Hobgood's seven stages of resistance: Maintaining → Reinforcing → Adapting → Revisioning → Retooling → Restructuring → Transforming. People will be at different stages at any given time — name where you are.

### Facilitative leadership (pp.26–27)
- *"Resist the expectation that you will take total responsibility for both understanding the problem and developing the solution."*
- *"Don't move too quickly to reassure people and lessen their concerns and anxiety. People need to see the potential threats before them."*
- *"Resist the pressure to restore order immediately by returning to the way things have always been done."*
- Provide silence and journaling time after each question so quieter voices have a chance to think before talkative ones fill the silence.

### Selling the process (pp.27–28)
- Start with leadership. *"Without the support of at least three-quarters of the leadership, it is difficult to initiate anything new in congregational life."*
- "Leadership" includes informal influencers (former leaders, deeply respected members), not just elected board.
- If key individuals are boycotting or speaking against the work — *"take a step back and do more of the marketing work."*

### Planning the process — critical elements (pp.28–30)

> Quoted nearly verbatim from p.28:

- **Permit enough time** to engage the widest possible participation.
- **Include a variety of opportunities** for input and involvement (different times, days, formats — accommodate seniors, parents, youth, shift workers).
- **Keep extensive notes** throughout the process.
- **Use visual and audiovisual presentations** (auditory, visual, kinesthetic learners).
- **Plan informal social events** as part of the process.
- **Celebrate the milestones** of the process.
- **View the production of the statements not as the end but as the first stage** of moving the congregation into a new way of being.

> *"Remember to include ways for the young people to be involved. They can be gathered together and asked what they think is most important about church, or they can be invited to draw pictures of what they'd like to see added to the church. Don't assume that the adults have all the good ideas!"* — p.30

### Creating the vision — three sample programs (pp.32–40)

**Program 1: Guided imagery for a group** (pp.33–36) — the closest match to our planned Oct 18 work. Five steps: (1) guided visualization of CUUC in 5–10 years, (2) jot down individual reactions, (3) share in groups of 3, (4) whole-group harvest of common + unique themes, (5) hand off to drafting team. Optional dot-voting on values as a supplement.

**Program 2: Individual reflection** (pp.37–38) — month-long meditation/reflection in worship, then small-group sharing.

**Program 3: Time line** (pp.39–40) — historical timeline of the congregation, then small-group reflection on values that have run through CUUC's life. Useful for grounding aspiration in heritage.

> *"Whichever method is used, make sure that it is easy for people to take part in by giving plenty of advance notice and providing child care and children's activities."* — p.32
>
> *"If exercises take longer than you originally thought they would, stop and talk with the members about that fact. Then work out a way you can continue the process, either through having additional sessions or perhaps agreeing to allow a smaller group to complete the activity and bring the results back for the larger group's input and feedback."* — p.32

### Criteria for a good vision statement (p.41)

A vision statement should be:

- Faithful to the congregation's best understanding of its religious heritage.
- Future-oriented (a statement of desired future).
- Appropriate to the congregation's history, culture, size, resources, location, capacities, and what members care deeply about.
- Realistic in terms of social context.
- Containing both **judgment and promise** — articulating the *different* future it wishes to create.
- A shared image growing out of the stated wishes and desires of the membership.
- Specific enough for direction; broad enough for inclusivity.

### Following through (pp.59+)

> *"The reason why most vision, mission, and covenant statements fail is because the congregation fails to plan for their effective implementation."* — p.59

After adoption, plan for:

- Liturgical use (read in worship).
- Governance integration (committee goals tied to vision).
- Budget alignment (mission-based budgeting).
- Visibility (website, sanctuary, order of service).
- Stewardship (pledge campaign grounded in vision).
- **Mission objectives** — 3–4 SMART objectives over the next 5 years (or stagger one per year).
- **Periodic revisiting** — at least every 5 years.

## The booklet's most useful single sentence

> *"Whatever process the team chooses, it should be open to modification and should provide for the eventual acceptance of the statements by the membership."* — p.29

## How this booklet differs from the seven LeaderLab pages we already have

The LeaderLab pages distill the booklet's main framing (and largely *replace* its theological/historical chapters), but they thin out on the operational details. The booklet uniquely contributes:

- **Three concrete vision-creation programs** (LeaderLab gives only sketches).
- **Hobgood's seven stages of resistance** to name out loud during the process.
- **Explicit warning about treating adaptive challenges as technical**.
- **The "selling" stage** as a distinct, named step worth time.
- **The "following through" failure mode** as the leading cause of statement death.
- **The intergenerational tip** — which validates our impulse to add youth programming.
- **The "rolled-up newsprint in a closet" image** — a vivid reminder of what failure looks like.
