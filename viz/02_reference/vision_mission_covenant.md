Can we look at the etymology of these 3 words to feel even more intuitively why we use them to describe the 3 fundamental documents? 

Vision: A carefully defined picture of where the congregation wants to be in
five or more years. It is the dream of what the congregation can become.

Mission: A concise statement of what the congregation wants to be known
for, or known as, within the wider world; what the congregation wants to
mean to the community.

Covenant: A statement of how members of the congregation will be with,
and will behave toward, one another, as well as what is promised or vowed
to one another and to the congregation as a whole.

The etymology makes the three documents feel like they answer three different deep questions:

**Vision = what we see.**
**Mission = what we are sent to do.**
**Covenant = how we come together and bind ourselves to one another.**

## 1. Vision — from “seeing”

**Vision** comes through Old French from Latin *visionem*, meaning something like “act of seeing, sight, thing seen.” Over time it came to mean not only physical sight, but also a vivid mental picture or foresight. ([Etymology Online][1])

So a congregational **vision** is not first a task list. It is a **shared act of seeing**.

It asks:

> What future can we see together clearly enough that it begins to call us forward?

That makes your definition feel very natural:

> “A carefully defined picture of where the congregation wants to be in five or more years.”

The key word is **picture**. A vision should be almost imaginable: “I can see us becoming that.” It is the dream made visible.

## 2. Mission — from “sending”

**Mission** comes from Latin *missio*, from *mittere*, “to send.” Its later sense includes “that for which one is sent or commissioned.” ([Etymology Online][2])

So a congregational **mission** is not mainly “what we like about ourselves.” It is:

> What are we sent into the world to do, offer, embody, or make possible?

That fits your definition beautifully:

> “What the congregation wants to be known for, or known as, within the wider world; what the congregation wants to mean to the community.”

The mission is the congregation facing **outward**. It answers: when we leave our building, our committee meetings, our internal concerns, what are we here to carry into the world?

A mission has movement in it. It is not just identity; it is **identity in motion**.

## 3. Covenant — from “coming together”

**Covenant** comes from Old French *covenant/convenant*, meaning “agreement, pact, promise,” ultimately from Latin *convenire*: “to come together, unite, agree,” from *com-* “together” + *venire* “to come.” ([Etymology Online][3])

That is almost perfect for congregational life.

A **covenant** is not merely a rulebook. It is a statement of how we agree to **come together** and remain together, especially when things are difficult.

It asks:

> What promises make us a people, rather than just a collection of individuals?

That fits your definition:

> “How members of the congregation will be with, and behave toward, one another… what is promised or vowed to one another and to the congregation as a whole.”

The etymology helps distinguish covenant from policy. A policy says, “Here are the procedures.” A covenant says, “Here is how we promise to be with one another while we pursue what matters.”

## The intuitive triad

You might summarize the three like this:

| Document     | Deep image                  | Direction        | Core question                                       |
| ------------ | --------------------------- | ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Vision**   | Seeing                      | Forward          | What future do we see ourselves becoming?           |
| **Mission**  | Sending                     | Outward          | What are we here to offer the world?                |
| **Covenant** | Coming together / promising | Inward and among | How do we bind ourselves to one another on the way? |

Or even more compactly:

> **Vision sees the future. Mission sends us into the world. Covenant binds us to one another for the journey.**

For a UU congregation, that feels especially right: vision without mission can become fantasy; mission without covenant can become burnout or conflict; covenant without vision and mission can become mere niceness or process. The three need one another.
