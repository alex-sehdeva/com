# Prior Visioning Processes (How Other Congregations Did It)

Two case studies, drawn from Tricia's prep work, of how other congregations actually ran a visioning process. Useful as comparative reference when designing CUUC's process.

## Case 1 — Workshop + dot-voting + discernment (origin unspecified, 2006)

> This Vision statement was an outcome of the Visioning process begun at a workshop in February 2006 and then distilled from the results of dot-voting. Raw data was transformed into the Vision Statement by **discernment of the underlying principles**. As one example, while "Expanded Facilities" was a key outcome of the workshop, our discernment of the results was that Facilities were not the goal, but a **means to the goal** of being able to house and support our programs and activities.
>
> Also two major categories were identified which encompassed the individual elements: **Our Community** and **Connection and Engagement**.

**Key takeaway:** the drafting team's job is not to transcribe what people said — it is to discern *what people are reaching toward through* what they said. "Expanded facilities" → "house and support our programs" is a representative move.

## Case 2 — UU Church of Palo Alto, CA (Sept 2020 onward)

> In late September 2020, Rev. Linda Thomson, a Congregational Life leader with the CUC, facilitated **two visioning workshops via Zoom**. Almost 50 of you attended at least one workshop. We captured what you said in **18 pages of notes**.
>
> You had many insights, which we reviewed intently with several results:
>
> - **Word cloud / word clusters** — extracted key aspirational words and phrases.
> - **Desirable-things capture** — many things you said you value, aspire to, and dream of.
> - **Detailed narrative** — material from the notes organized under linked headings, in a longer format with quotes and a shorter highlights version.
>
> Then, to summarize, we asked, based on your insights, **"What are we called to be and do?"**
>
> Next, we held several **Visioning Labs**, at which we asked committee representatives to comment on the one-page results from the above three approaches. Based on that feedback, we developed one of the approaches into a possible long-form mission statement, containing **three potential strategic directions** to guide our church over the next few years. *What We Aspire to Be and Do* represents our progress to date.

**Key takeaway:** Palo Alto is the closest analogue to what CUUC is contemplating. They:

1. Held two input workshops (CUUC: tentatively one service, 2026-10-18).
2. Triangulated the raw input through three different distillation approaches — not just one.
3. Ran "Visioning Labs" with committee representatives between raw input and final draft — a structured intermediate review.
4. Used the question **"What are we called to be and do?"** as the synthesis prompt.

## Implications for CUUC's process

- Plan for **multiple distillation approaches** on the same raw input (cards, themes, word clusters). The one that fits best will reveal itself.
- Build in at least one **intermediate review** with COM / drafters before the candidate statement(s) go to the congregation.
- Steal the framing question: *"What are we called to be and do?"*
- Be ready to **discern means vs. ends** — the drafting team should distinguish what people *literally* asked for from what they were *reaching toward*.
